OnReason method

We ask whether confidence matches the evidence.

OnReason critiques apologetics podcasts by reconstructing their strongest claims, tracing the evidence actually supplied, and lowering or raising confidence in proportion to that evidence. The aim is not dismissal. The aim is calibration.

1. Charity First

Steelman before critique

Each page tries to state the episode's argument in its best reasonable form before evaluating it. We do not score points by weakening the claim, mocking the speaker, or replacing the argument with an easier target.

2. Evidence Next

Separate assertion from support

A transcript may contain testimony, analogies, proof-texts, historical examples, moral intuitions, or expert claims. Those can matter, but they are not all the same kind of evidence and they do not support every conclusion with equal strength.

3. Symmetry Always

Apply the same standard to rivals

If a standard would make a Christian claim plausible, we ask whether it would also make a rival religious, secular, psychological, or social explanation plausible. Special permission for the favored view is a warning sign.

4. Proportion Finally

Let belief come in degrees

The result is rarely simply true or false. A claim may be interesting, pastorally useful, morally serious, or historically suggestive while still falling short of the confidence placed on it.

Workflow

How a critique page is built

  1. Collect the transcript. The private corpus keeps podcast assets separated by show so public pages can cite short excerpts without publishing full transcripts.
  2. Identify substantive claims. Low-content banter, announcements, and repeated setup are skipped. Claims that carry historical, moral, theological, evidential, or public-policy weight are retained.
  3. Reconstruct the best argument. The page states what the claim would need to mean in order to be worth taking seriously.
  4. Anchor the claim in the transcript. Short quotations and timestamp ranges show where the claim comes from without turning the public page into a transcript republication.
  5. Map the claim to tests. Each section connects the episode's claim to relevant Free of Faith, LogFall, CogBias, and local audit frameworks.
  6. Name what would change our mind. Every critique should say what evidence would raise confidence and what would lower it.

Framework

The five recurring tests

Calibration

Does confidence rise only as far as the evidence actually warrants?

Symmetry

Would the same reasoning also help a rival worldview or explanation?

Architecture

Does the argument supply the missing moral, historical, or theological structure it needs?

Alternatives

Have secular, pluralist, psychological, social, and theological rivals been compared fairly?

Bounded Agency

Does concern become proportionate action where agency exists, rather than inflated responsibility?

Philosophy

What the critiques are, and are not

They are audits of public warrant.

OnReason asks what a listener who does not already grant the speaker's theological assumptions has been given as evidence. Insider claims can be meaningful inside a tradition, but public confidence needs public warrant.

They are not attempts to erase every insight.

A critique can affirm practical wisdom, emotional force, moral seriousness, or a helpful distinction while still saying the larger apologetic conclusion has not been earned.

Source Practice

How public sources are used

Public critique pages cite official episode pages, short transcript excerpts, and external reasoning tools such as Free of Faith, LogFall, and CogBias. The private transcript corpus remains separate from the public site. That separation keeps the pages focused on analysis rather than redistribution.
